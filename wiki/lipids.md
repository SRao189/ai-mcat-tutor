# Lipids

## Lipid roles hydrophobicity and fatty acids

Lipids are oily or fatty substances with three primary physiological roles. In adipose cells, triglycerides store energy. In cellular membranes, phospholipids form a barrier between intracellular and extracellular environments. Cholesterol is a special lipid that serves as the building block for hydrophobic steroid hormones.

The defining feature of lipids is hydrophobicity. Hydrophobic substances do not dissolve well in water because carbon-carbon bonds and carbon-hydrogen bonds are nonpolar. Hydrophilic substances dissolve well in water, while hydrophobic or lipophilic substances avoid water.

Fatty acids are long unsubstituted alkanes ending in a carboxylic acid. The chain is typically 14 to 18 carbons long, and human cells predominantly synthesize even-numbered fatty acids because they are made two carbons at a time from acetate. A fatty acid with no carbon-carbon double bonds is saturated. Unsaturated fatty acids have one or more double bonds in the tail, and these double bonds are almost always cis, which bends or kinks the chain.

Free fatty acids in water form micelles. Their hydrophobic tails cluster inside the sphere, while polar or charged carboxyl head groups face water. The force driving tails toward the center is the hydrophobic interaction.

## Triacylglycerols and energy storage

The storage form of fatty acids is fat, technically called triacylglycerol or triglyceride. A triglyceride is composed of three fatty acids esterified to glycerol. Glycerol is a three-carbon triol with three hydroxyl groups that can be esterified to fatty acids. Fatty acids are stored as relatively inert fat because free fatty acids are reactive chemicals.

Triacylglycerols undergo reactions typical of esters, including base-catalyzed hydrolysis. Soaps are sodium salts of fatty acids and are amphipathic, meaning they have both hydrophilic and hydrophobic regions. Soap can be made by base-catalyzed hydrolysis of triglycerides from animal fat into fatty acid salts, a reaction called saponification. Lipases are enzymes that hydrolyze fats.

Triacylglycerols are stored in fat cells as an energy source. Fats are more efficient energy storage molecules than carbohydrates for two reasons: packing and energy content. Their hydrophobicity allows fats to pack more closely than carbohydrates, which carry substantial water of solvation. Fat molecules also store more energy carbon-for-carbon than carbohydrates because fats are more reduced. Since energy metabolism begins with oxidation of foodstuffs, more reduced fats release more energy when oxidized.

## Phospholipids membranes and fluidity

Membrane lipids are phospholipids, also called phosphatides, derived from diacylglycerol phosphate. The phosphate group often has larger polar molecules attached, such as choline, ethanolamine, or inositol. Phosphatidylcholine and phosphatidylethanolamine are the most common phospholipids in eukaryotic cells. Phosphatidylcholine is also a major lipid component of lung surfactant, and phosphatidylinositol participates in signal transmission across cell membranes.

Phospholipids minimize interactions with water by forming lipid bilayers. Hydrophobic interactions drive bilayer formation, and van der Waals forces between long tails stabilize the formed bilayer.

Unsaturation in phospholipid fatty acid tails increases membrane fluidity because double bonds disrupt orderly packing of hydrophobic tails. Decreasing fatty acid tail length also increases fluidity. Cholesterol is a third modulator of membrane fluidity: at low temperatures it increases fluidity, while at high temperatures it reduces fluidity. The structural determinants of membrane fluidity are degree of saturation, tail length, and amount of cholesterol.

The lipid bilayer separates the cell interior from the exterior and also participates in regulation and signaling. The plasma membrane is impermeable to charged particles such as sodium ions, so protein channels are required for ions to enter or leave cells. Charged peptide hormones cannot pass through the membrane and instead bind cell-surface receptors that transmit signals into the cell.

## Terpenes steroids and steroid hormones

Terpenes are built from isoprene units with the general formula (C5H8)n. Terpenes may be linear or cyclic and are classified by the number of isoprene units they contain. Monoterpenes have two isoprene units, sesquiterpenes have three, diterpenes have four, and squalene is a triterpene with six isoprene units.

Squalene is biosynthetically used to make steroids and is also a component of earwax. Functionalized species built from an isoprene skeleton and containing other elements are terpenoids; vitamin A is one example.

Steroids are hydrophobic lipids with a characteristic tetracyclic ring system based on cholesterol. Cholesterol is an amphipathic steroid, an important component of lipid bilayers, and is both obtained from the diet and synthesized in the liver. Cholesterol is carried in blood packaged with fats and proteins into lipoproteins.

Steroid hormones are made from cholesterol. Testosterone and estradiol are examples. Because steroids are highly hydrophobic, they can diffuse through the lipid bilayer into the cytoplasm. Steroid hormone receptors are located inside cells rather than on the cell surface, in contrast to peptide hormones such as insulin.

## Other lipid classes and fat-soluble vitamins

Sphingolipids are structured similarly to phospholipids, but their backbone is sphingosine instead of glycerol. The significant sphingolipid in humans identified by the source is sphingomyelin, an important component of the myelin sheath around neurons.

Waxes are long-chain fats esterified to long-chain alcohols. They are extremely hydrophobic and often form waterproof protective barriers, including plant barriers and animal earwax.

Fat-soluble vitamins are absorbed with dietary fat and stored in adipose tissue and in the liver. The four fat-soluble vitamins are A, D, E, and K, and all have ring structures. Vitamin A is a terpenoid important for vision, growth, epithelial maintenance, and immune function. Vitamin D is derived from cholesterol and regulates blood levels of calcium and phosphate. Vitamin E compounds, called tocopherols, act as antioxidants. Vitamin K is an important coenzyme in clotting-protein activation.

Prostaglandins are eicosanoids derived from 20-carbon fatty acids. They can regulate smooth muscle contraction, blood vessel diameter, and gastric integrity, depending on the receptor to which they bind.

## Fatty acid oxidation and ketogenesis

After initial fat digestion, chylomicrons made of fat and lipoprotein are transported through the lymphatic system and bloodstream to organs. Dietary triacylglycerol is hydrolyzed to free fatty acids, which can undergo beta-oxidation.

Fatty acid oxidation begins at the outer mitochondrial membrane with activation of the fatty acid. Acyl-CoA synthetase catalyzes this reaction, which invests two ATP equivalents to generate fatty acyl-CoA for transport into the mitochondrion. In the matrix, fatty acyl-CoA undergoes a repeated four-reaction series that cleaves the bond between the alpha and beta carbons, releases acetyl-CoA, and generates one FADH2 and one NADH per round. Each round of beta-oxidation removes a two-carbon acetyl-CoA, and the final round cleaves a four-carbon fatty acyl-CoA to produce two acetyl-CoA.

For lauric acid, a 12-carbon saturated fatty acid, complete beta-oxidation requires two ATP equivalents for activation and five rounds of beta-oxidation. This generates five FADH2, five NADH, and six acetyl-CoA, which enter the Krebs cycle; after the electron transport chain and subtracting activation cost, the source gives 78 ATP from lauric acid.

During starvation, glycogen stores become exhausted and blood glucose falls. The liver generates ketone bodies in the mitochondrial matrix by ketogenesis to help supply the central nervous system when glucose is scarce. Ketone bodies are made from acetyl-CoA and include acetone, acetoacetate, and beta-hydroxybutyrate. They cross the blood-brain barrier and can be converted back to acetyl-CoA in target organs so acetyl-CoA can enter the Krebs cycle.

Ketogenesis can also occur when glucose is present in blood but cannot enter cells, such as prolonged insulin absence in type I diabetes. In that case cells rely on fatty acid oxidation for acetyl-CoA, high acetyl-CoA levels are converted to ketone bodies, and acidic ketone bodies can cause diabetic ketoacidosis.

## Fatty acid synthesis and metabolic state

Fatty acid catabolism occurs in the mitochondrial matrix, while fatty acid synthesis occurs in the cytoplasm. Synthesis repeatedly adds two-carbon subunits to a growing fatty acid chain. Before building the chain, acetyl-CoA is activated by carboxylation to malonyl-CoA. This committed step requires ATP and is catalyzed by acetyl-CoA carboxylase.

Fatty acid synthase is a large enzyme with multiple catalytic domains. The growing chain is carried by acyl carrier protein, and malonyl-CoA condenses with the chain as carbon dioxide is lost. The process requires the reducing power of NADPH, generally obtained from the pentose phosphate pathway. The cycle repeats until a 16-carbon fatty acid is generated, after which additional enzymes can modify or elongate it.

Cells generally prefer carbohydrates as fuel. When blood sugar is high, cells take up glucose and make ATP by glycolysis; liver and muscle store glucose as glycogen, and the liver can convert acetyl-CoA to fatty acids that become triglycerides. When blood sugar falls, the liver breaks down glycogen and begins gluconeogenesis. If starvation continues after glycogen stores are used, triglycerides from adipose tissue are broken down into free fatty acids and glycerol, cells run beta-oxidation, glycerol can support gluconeogenesis, and some acetyl-CoA is converted into ketone bodies.

Proteins and amino acids can also be used as fuel. The amino group can be removed and used for nitrogenous compounds or converted into urea, while the carbon skeleton can be broken down into water and carbon dioxide or converted to glucose through glucogenic amino acids or acetyl-CoA through ketogenic amino acids.
