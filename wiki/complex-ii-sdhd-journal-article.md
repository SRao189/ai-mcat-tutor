# Complex II SDHD Journal Article

## Background and Citation

This Journal Article Exercise analyzes Aloka B. Bandara, Joshua C. Drake, and David A. Brown, "Complex II subunit SDHD is critical for cell growth and metabolism, which can be partially restored with a synthetic ubiquinone analog," published in BMC Molecular and Cell Biology, volume 22, article 35, in 2021.

Complex II, also called succinate dehydrogenase, links the Krebs cycle and the electron transport chain. In the Krebs cycle it catalyzes oxidation of succinate to fumarate. In the electron transport chain it accepts electrons from succinate and donates them to ubiquinone. The SDHC and SDHD subunits anchor Complex II in the inner mitochondrial membrane and form the CoQ binding site. The article frames SDHD dysfunction as useful to model because SDHD helps anchor Complex II, transfer electrons to CoQ, and has been connected with paragangliomas and pheochromocytomas.

## Study Methods

The investigators used CRISPR/Cas9 to mutate SDHD in HEK293 cells. They validated the HEK293 Delta SDHD mutant by Western immunoblotting, PCR amplification and sequencing around the CRISPR target site, and sequencing of the top three predicted off-target sites. They also measured representative subunits from all five electron transport chain complexes.

The study compared parent HEK293 cells with HEK293 Delta SDHD cells. It measured ROS production, apoptosis, necrosis, cell growth, doubling time, oxygen consumption, glycolysis by extracellular acidification rate, ATP synthesis, and the effect of idebenone treatment on respiration and growth. Student's t-tests were used, and mean differences were considered significant at p < 0.05.

## Results and Discussion

The mutant was missing the expected SDHD-associated Western blot bands, and sequencing showed an INDEL near the CRISPR target site that altered the downstream amino acid sequence and introduced a stop codon. The top predicted off-target regions matched the parent cell line in the source text.

The SDHD mutant had lower expression of SDHB, NDUFB8, MT-CO1, and ATP5A, while UQCRC2 was not different between parent and mutant cells. The article interprets this as evidence that SDHD mutation affected Complex II and was associated with changes in expression of several other electron transport chain complexes, while not altering the measured Complex III subunit.

Compared with parent HEK293 cells, HEK293 Delta SDHD cells showed increased apoptosis and necrosis at the measured time points. ROS was higher immediately after substrate addition but lower at later time points. The mutant cells also grew more slowly, had fewer recovered cells after four days, and had impaired oxygen consumption, basal respiration, reserve respiratory capacity, maximal respiratory capacity, glycolysis, glycolytic capacity, and ATP synthesis.

Idebenone was tested as a synthetic ubiquinone analog. In permeabilized mutant cells, 1 micromolar idebenone increased basal respiration and maximal respiratory capacity but did not improve reserve respiratory capacity. In the O2k respirometry assay, later 5 micromolar idebenone additions and ADP were associated with significant improvements compared with DMSO control. In the growth assay, 1 micromolar idebenone increased recovered mutant cell number after 10 days, 5 micromolar did not improve growth, and 10 micromolar caused cell death according to the article.

The discussion argues that SDHD mutation disrupted respiration throughout the electron transport chain, suppressed glycolysis and ATP synthesis, limited growth, and increased apoptosis and necrosis. The authors hypothesize that SDHD mutation may disrupt the Krebs cycle and reduce NADH and FADH2 donation to the electron transport chain. They conclude that the mutant model may be valuable for testing mitochondrial-focused therapeutics and for studying mechanisms regulated by Complex II function.

## ORCWS: Organism and Organization

The Princeton Review solution identifies the experimental system as a human cell line: human embryonic kidney HEK293 cells. The experimental manipulation was knockout of the Complex II SDHD gene, followed by comparison of mitochondrial respiration and related outcomes against the parent cell line.

The exercise identifies the CRISPR/Cas9-created knockout line as the unusual feature of the organisms. It treats the study as having two main groups: HEK293 parent cells and HEK293 Delta SDHD cells. It also characterizes the exercise as a short-term study with multiple measured time points, including measurements through 72 hours for some assays.

## ORCWS: Results

The Princeton Review solution says the results are presented as graphs, DNA sequence images, and gel images. It identifies time and presence or absence of SDHD as the independent variables. It identifies cell growth, ROS levels, apoptosis, necrosis, oxygen consumption, glycolysis, and ATP synthesis as dependent variables.

For critical analysis, the result pattern is that mutant HEK293 Delta SDHD cells differed from parent HEK293 cells in growth and metabolism. The solution describes decreased cell numbers and increased doubling time in the mutant as a correlation, and describes the comparison between parent and mutant cells for ROS, apoptosis, and necrosis as supporting cause-and-effect reasoning within the experimental design.

## ORCWS: Control and Comparison

The control group is the parent HEK293 cell line. The experimental group is HEK293 Delta SDHD. The source solution states that the parent cells have SDHD intact, while the experimental cells lack functional SDHD after CRISPR/Cas9 mutation.

The exercise identifies only one main experimental group for the basic mutant comparison. It also notes that the study was not blinded. For idebenone experiments, DMSO-treated mutant cells served as a vehicle comparison for idebenone-treated mutant cells in the respiration and growth assays.

## ORCWS: Weirdness

The Princeton Review solution flags one unexpected pattern: 5 micromolar idebenone treatment was less effective than 1 micromolar idebenone in the long-term growth experiment. The article reports that 1 micromolar idebenone increased recovered mutant cells after 10 days, while 5 micromolar idebenone did not improve growth.

This is a useful MCAT-style "weirdness" point because a higher dose is not automatically more effective. The data require comparing the actual treatment groups rather than assuming a monotonic dose response.

## ORCWS: Statistics

The study used statistical testing and presented p-values, error bars, and asterisks. The methods state that Student's t-tests were performed and that p < 0.05 was the threshold for statistical significance.

The Princeton Review solution identifies examples of non-significant data: the Complex III subunit comparison between HEK293 and HEK293 Delta SDHD cells, and the comparison between DMSO and 5 micromolar idebenone treatment in the growth experiment. These examples matter because not every measured difference in a figure should be treated as statistically supported.

## Interpreting the Data

A strong interpretation stays close to the comparison being made. Parent HEK293 cells provide the baseline for asking what changes when SDHD is disrupted. DMSO-treated mutant cells provide the baseline for asking whether idebenone changes mutant-cell outcomes.

The article supports the conclusion that SDHD is important for mitochondrial respiration, ATP synthesis, glycolysis-associated measurements, and cell growth in this in vitro system. It also supports the narrower conclusion that idebenone partially restored selected mutant phenotypes, not that it fully corrected all defects. A next critical-analysis step would be to ask whether the same patterns hold in additional cell types, in rescue experiments that restore SDHD, or in longer-term and in vivo settings.

## Final Summary

Bandara, Drake, and Brown created a CRISPR/Cas9 HEK293 Delta SDHD model to test how loss of the Complex II SDHD subunit affects cell growth and metabolism. Compared with parent HEK293 cells, the mutant showed disrupted Complex II-related protein expression, broad impairment of respiration, reduced glycolysis-associated measurements and ATP synthesis, slower growth, and increased apoptosis and necrosis.

The article then tested idebenone as a synthetic ubiquinone analog. Idebenone partially improved selected respiration and growth outcomes in the mutant, with 1 micromolar improving long-term recovered cell number while 5 micromolar did not. The ORCWS analysis emphasizes identifying the cell-line comparison, reading graphs and p-values carefully, noticing non-significant results, and not assuming that a higher dose is always better.
