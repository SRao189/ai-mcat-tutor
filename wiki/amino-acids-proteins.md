# Amino Acids and Proteins

## Amino acid structure and side-chain categories

Proteins are biological macromolecules that can act as enzymes, hormones, receptors, channels, transporters, antibodies, and support structures. Proteins are composed of 20 different amino acids linked together in polymers, and the composition and sequence of amino acids in the polypeptide chain make each protein unique.

All 20 amino acids share the same nitrogen-carbon-carbon backbone. Each amino acid has a tetrahedral alpha-carbon connected to an amino group, a carboxyl group, hydrogen, and a variable R-group. The R-group gives each amino acid the physical and chemical properties that distinguish it from the other amino acids.

Amino acids are organized into broad categories based on side-chain chemistry: acidic, basic, nonpolar, polar, sulfur-containing, and proline. Aspartic acid and glutamic acid have acidic carboxylic acid side chains and are observed as aspartate and glutamate at physiological pH. Lysine, arginine, and histidine have basic side chains; lysine and arginine are cationic at physiological pH, while histidine has a side-chain pKa close to physiological pH and can be protonated or deprotonated.

Hydrophobic amino acids have aliphatic or aromatic side chains and tend to associate with each other rather than with water. They are therefore commonly found on the interior of folded globular proteins. Polar amino acids have R-groups that can form hydrogen bonds with water but do not act as acids or bases. Serine, threonine, and tyrosine hydroxyl groups can be modified by phosphorylation by kinases. Cysteine contains a thiol, methionine contains a thioether, and proline has its amino group covalently bound to its nonpolar side chain, creating a distinctive ring structure.

## Amino acid acid-base behavior and isoelectric point

Amino acids are amphoteric because they contain an acidic carboxylic acid group and a basic amino group. Carboxyl groups of amino acids generally have a pKa of about 2, while ammonium groups generally have a pKa of 9 or 10.

The Henderson-Hasselbalch relationship is pH = pKa + log([base form]/[acid form]). If the pH of the solution is less than the pKa of an acidic group, that group will mostly be protonated. If the pH is greater than the pKa, that group will mostly be deprotonated.

Every amino acid has a pH at which it has no overall net charge because positive and negative charges cancel. A molecule with balanced positive and negative charges is a dipolar ion or zwitterion, and the pH at which a molecule is uncharged is its isoelectric point, or pI. For a molecule with two functional groups, such as glycine, the pI is calculated by averaging the pKas of the two functional groups. The glycine titration example gives pKa values of 2.34 and 9.60, so the pI is 5.97.

## Peptide bonds and disulfide bonds

Polypeptides are formed by linking amino acids together in peptide bonds. A peptide bond is formed between the carboxyl group of one amino acid and the alpha-amino group of another amino acid with the loss of water. In a polypeptide chain, the repeating N-C-C pattern is the backbone, and an individual amino acid is called a residue when it is part of a polypeptide chain.

The amino terminus is the first end made during polypeptide synthesis, the carboxy terminus is made last, and by convention the amino-terminal residue is written first. Hydrolysis of a protein by another protein is proteolysis or proteolytic cleavage, and the enzyme that performs the cleavage is a protease.

Cysteine has a reactive thiol in its side chain. The thiol of one cysteine can react with the thiol of another cysteine to produce a covalent sulfur-sulfur bond called a disulfide bond. The cysteines forming a disulfide bond may be located in the same polypeptide chain or in different polypeptide chains. Disulfide bridges stabilize tertiary protein structure, and a cysteine residue becomes cystine after it is disulfide-bonded to another cysteine.

## Levels of protein structure and denaturation

Each protein folds into a unique three-dimensional structure required for function, and improperly folded or denatured proteins are nonfunctional. Denaturation disrupts a protein's shape without breaking peptide bonds. Proteins can be denatured by urea, extremes of pH, extremes of temperature, and changes in salt concentration.

Primary structure is the linear order of amino acid residues, which is the amino acid sequence. The peptide bond determines primary structure because it links one amino acid to the next in a polypeptide.

Secondary structure is the initial folding of a polypeptide into shapes stabilized by hydrogen bonds between backbone NH and CO groups. The two most common secondary structures are the alpha-helix and the beta-pleated sheet. Proline disrupts alpha-helices because it lacks the backbone N-H needed for hydrogen bonding and because its ring structure kinks the polypeptide chain. Beta-sheets are stabilized by hydrogen bonding between residues distant in the chain or on separate chains, with strands running parallel or antiparallel.

Tertiary structure involves interactions between amino acid residues distant from each other in the polypeptide chain. These interactions may include van der Waals forces between nonpolar side chains, hydrogen bonds between polar side chains, disulfide bonds between cysteine residues, and electrostatic interactions between acidic and basic side chains. Hydrophobic R-groups tend to fold into the interior of the protein, away from water, while hydrophilic R-groups tend to be exposed on the surface.

Quaternary structure describes interactions between separate polypeptide subunits in a multisubunit complex. The forces stabilizing quaternary structure are generally the same as those involved in tertiary structure, but peptide bonds do not define quaternary structure because peptide bonds define sequence.

## Enzymes reaction coupling and regulation

Enzymes are biological catalysts. They increase reaction rate by lowering activation energy, but they do not change the Delta G between reactants and products. Enzymes therefore have a kinetic role rather than a thermodynamic one, and they cannot control the direction in which a reaction proceeds.

Thermodynamically unfavorable cellular reactions can be driven forward by reaction coupling, in which one very favorable reaction drives an unfavorable one because free energy changes are additive. ATP hydrolysis is the favorable reaction cells use for coupling. Hydrolysis of one phosphate group from ATP has Delta G degrees prime of about -7.3 kcal/mol in the lab and actual cellular Delta G of about -12 kcal/mol.

Most enzymes are proteins that must fold into specific three-dimensional structures to act as catalysts. The active site is the region of an enzyme's three-dimensional structure directly involved in catalysis. The induced fit model states that substrate binding induces a conformational change in the enzyme, and enzymes accelerate reactions by stabilizing the transition state, lowering the activation energy barrier between reactants and products.

Enzyme activity is regulated by covalent modification, proteolytic cleavage, association with other polypeptides, and allosteric regulation. Phosphorylation of serine, threonine, or tyrosine residues can activate or inactivate an enzyme and is reversible by phosphatases. Zymogens are inactive enzyme forms that can be activated by proteolytic cleavage. Allosteric regulation occurs when a small molecule binds at a site distinct from the active site and changes active-site activity; the binding is generally noncovalent and reversible.

Feedback inhibition commonly shuts off an enzyme early in a pathway when an end product accumulates. Feedforward stimulation occurs when a substrate or a molecule used to synthesize the substrate stimulates an enzyme later in a pathway.

## Enzyme kinetics inhibition and Lineweaver-Burk plots

Enzyme kinetics studies the rate of product formation from substrate in the presence of an enzyme. Reaction rate V is the amount of product formed per unit time and depends on substrate concentration and enzyme concentration. At low substrate concentration, V is directly proportional to substrate concentration. At high substrate concentration, enzyme active sites become saturated, and adding more substrate no longer increases reaction rate. The saturated reaction rate is Vmax.

The Michaelis constant Km is the substrate concentration at which reaction velocity is half its maximum. Km is unique for each enzyme-substrate pair and gives information about enzyme affinity for substrate. A low Km means not much substrate is required to reach half the maximum rate, so the enzyme has high affinity for that substrate.

Many multisubunit enzymes show cooperativity, in which substrate binding to one subunit changes the affinity of other subunits for substrate. Positive cooperativity means substrate binding to one subunit increases the affinity of the other subunits, producing a sigmoidal curve. Negative cooperativity means substrate binding to one subunit reduces the affinity of the other subunits. Cooperativity is a special kind of allosteric interaction in which binding at one active site affects other active sites.

Competitive inhibitors compete with substrate for the active site; inhibition can be overcome by adding more substrate, so Vmax is unchanged and Km increases. Noncompetitive inhibitors bind at an allosteric site, cannot be displaced by adding substrate, decrease Vmax, and typically do not alter Km. Uncompetitive inhibitors bind only to the enzyme-substrate complex and decrease both Vmax and Km. Mixed-type inhibitors bind either free enzyme or the enzyme-substrate complex; they decrease Vmax and have variable effects on Km.

The Lineweaver-Burk plot is a double reciprocal plot of enzyme kinetics. The y-axis is 1/V, the x-axis is 1/[S], the slope is Km/Vmax, the y-intercept is 1/Vmax, and the x-intercept is -1/Km. A noncompetitive inhibitor leaves the x-intercept unchanged but decreases Vmax, increasing the y-intercept and the slope.
