# Carbohydrates and Carbohydrate Metabolism

## Monosaccharides disaccharides and glycosidic linkages

Carbohydrates generally serve as a principal energy source for cellular metabolism because they can be oxidized to carbon dioxide while releasing large amounts of energy. A single carbohydrate molecule is a monosaccharide, or simple sugar, and monosaccharides have the general formula CnH2nOn.

Two monosaccharides bonded together form a disaccharide. A few monosaccharides form an oligosaccharide, and many form a polysaccharide. The covalent bond between sugar molecules is a glycosidic linkage, formed by an enzyme-catalyzed dehydration reaction.

Glycosidic linkages are named by the carbons that form the linkage and by the alpha or beta configuration. Common MCAT disaccharides include sucrose, lactose, maltose, and cellobiose. The source lists sucrose as Glc-alpha-1,2-Fru, lactose as Gal-beta-1,4-Glc, maltose as Glc-alpha-1,4-Glc, and cellobiose as Glc-beta-1,4-Glc.

## Polysaccharides and glycosidic hydrolysis

Glycogen is an animal energy-storage carbohydrate made of thousands of glucose units joined mainly by alpha-1,4 linkages with alpha-1,6 branches. Starch serves a similar energy-storage role in plants. Cellulose is the polymerized form of cellobiose; its beta-glycosidic bonds allow cellulose to form long, straight, fibrous polymers found in wood and cotton.

Hydrolysis of polysaccharides into monosaccharides is thermodynamically favored and is needed before sugars can enter metabolic pathways such as glycolysis. The reaction does not occur at a significant rate without enzyme catalysis. Enzymes that hydrolyze sugars are highly specific for their linkages; maltase, for example, hydrolyzes maltose into two glucose monosaccharides.

This linkage specificity explains why humans can digest starch and glycogen but not cellulose. Mammalian enzymes generally cannot break the beta-glycosidic linkages in cellulose. Lactase is an exception because it can digest lactose, which has a beta linkage; people without lactase are lactose malabsorbers and may become lactose intolerant when bacteria in the colon metabolize the undigested lactose.

## Cellular respiration overview and glycolysis

When glucose is oxidized, most energy is first captured by reducing high-energy electron carriers rather than by making ATP directly. NAD+ and FAD accept high-energy electrons to become NADH and FADH2, then later deliver those electrons to the electron transport chain so a proton gradient can be used to make ATP.

Glucose oxidation proceeds through four stages: glycolysis, the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, the Krebs cycle, and electron transport with oxidative phosphorylation. Glycolysis occurs in the cytoplasm and does not require oxygen. It splits one glucose into two pyruvate molecules and produces a net surplus of 2 ATP and 2 NADH per glucose.

The overall glycolysis relationship from the source is: glucose + 2 ADP + 2 Pi + 2 NAD+ -> 2 pyruvate + 2 ATP + 2 NADH + 2 H2O + 2 H+. ATP is initially invested to phosphorylate glucose and fructose-6-phosphate before later substrate-level phosphorylation generates ATP.

Phosphofructokinase, or PFK, catalyzes the formation of fructose-1,6-bisphosphate from fructose-6-phosphate. This step is thermodynamically very favorable, practically irreversible, and is the committed step of glycolysis. PFK is an important allosteric regulatory point; high ATP inhibits PFK, while elevated AMP activates it.

## Fermentation and the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex

Under aerobic conditions, pyruvate from glycolysis enters the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex and the Krebs cycle, while NADH and FADH2 are reoxidized by electron transport using oxygen as the final electron acceptor. Under anaerobic conditions, electron transport cannot function, NAD+ becomes limiting, and glycolysis would stop without NAD+ regeneration.

Fermentation regenerates NAD+ under anaerobic conditions so glycolysis can continue. It uses pyruvate as the electron acceptor for NADH. Yeast can reduce pyruvate to ethanol, while human muscle cells can reduce pyruvate to lactate during strenuous exercise when oxygen delivery cannot keep up with demand.

The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex, or PDC, operates in the mitochondrial matrix. It oxidatively decarboxylates pyruvate, converting a 3-carbon molecule into an activated 2-carbon acetyl group attached to coenzyme A, while releasing carbon dioxide and producing NADH. Loaded coenzyme A is acetyl-CoA, and its high-energy thioester bond helps transfer the acetyl fragment into the Krebs cycle.

The PDC contains a thiamine pyrophosphate prosthetic group. The alpha-ketoglutarate dehydrogenase complex in the Krebs cycle is similar because it also uses a TPP prosthetic group and catalyzes oxidative decarboxylation. A low-energy charge, reflected by a high AMP or ADP to ATP ratio, stimulates the PDC.

## Krebs cycle and oxidative phosphorylation

The Krebs cycle takes the 2-carbon acetyl unit from acetyl-CoA, condenses it with oxaloacetate, and releases two carbon dioxide molecules while generating NADH and FADH2. It is also called the tricarboxylic acid cycle or citric acid cycle. Per glucose, the Krebs cycle produces 6 NADH, 2 FADH2, and 2 GTP; pyruvate dehydrogenase produces 2 NADH per glucose before the cycle.

The pyruvate dehydrogenase complex and the Krebs cycle occur in the mitochondrial matrix in eukaryotes. Electron transport and ATP synthase are located in the inner mitochondrial membrane. The inner membrane is highly impermeable to protons, and electron transport pumps protons from the matrix into the intermembrane space.

Oxidative phosphorylation is the oxidation of NADH and FADH2 coupled to phosphorylation of ADP to ATP. Electrons enter the electron transport chain through NADH dehydrogenase or through ubiquinone, pass through carriers including cytochrome C, and ultimately reduce oxygen to water at cytochrome C oxidase. The proton gradient powers ATP synthase as protons flow back into the matrix.

The chapter gives approximate ATP yields of 2.5 ATP per mitochondrial NADH and 1.5 ATP per FADH2. Cytosolic NADH from glycolysis usually yields 1.5 ATP in eukaryotes when electrons enter through the glycerol phosphate shuttle. The source gives a theoretical maximum of 30 ATP per glucose in eukaryotes and 32 ATP per glucose in prokaryotes.

## Gluconeogenesis and reciprocal regulation

Gluconeogenesis occurs when dietary glucose is unavailable and liver glycogen stores are depleted. It occurs primarily in the liver and to a lesser extent in the kidneys. It converts non-carbohydrate precursors such as lactate, pyruvate, Krebs cycle intermediates, glycerol, and the carbon skeletons of most amino acids into glucose.

Gluconeogenesis uses many of the same enzymes as glycolysis, but the enzymes that catalyze irreversible glycolysis steps are replaced. Pyruvate carboxylase converts pyruvate to oxaloacetate using ATP. Phosphoenolpyruvate carboxykinase converts oxaloacetate to PEP using GTP. Fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase removes phosphate from fructose-1,6-bisphosphate, and glucose-6-phosphatase produces free glucose that can leave liver cells.

Altogether, gluconeogenesis requires six high-energy phosphate bonds, four ATP and two GTP, and two NADH. Acetyl-CoA cannot participate in gluconeogenesis, which helps explain why free fatty acids cannot be converted to glucose during starvation, while triglyceride glycerol can.

Glycolysis and gluconeogenesis are reciprocally regulated to prevent futile cycling. PFK promotes glycolysis, while fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase promotes gluconeogenesis. In energy-starved states, elevated AMP activates PFK and inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase, enhancing glycolysis and suppressing gluconeogenesis.

Fructose-2,6-bisphosphate also stimulates PFK and inhibits fructose-1,6-bisphosphatase. When blood glucose is high, insulin stimulates formation of fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, activating glycolysis and turning off gluconeogenesis. When blood glucose is low, glucagon triggers breakdown of fructose-2,6-bisphosphate, inhibiting glycolysis and stimulating gluconeogenesis.

## Glycogen metabolism

Glycogen is a polymer of glucose found in muscle and liver cells and is the main carbohydrate storage form in animals. Glycogenesis starts with glucose-6-phosphate, which is converted to glucose-1-phosphate by phosphoglucomutase. Glucose-1-phosphate is activated with UTP to form UDP-glucose, and glycogen synthase adds UDP-glucose to the growing glycogen polymer.

Glycogenolysis removes glucose units from glycogen by phosphorylation, producing glucose-1-phosphate. Glucose-1-phosphate is converted to glucose-6-phosphate, which can reenter glycolysis. To release glucose into the bloodstream, liver cells must dephosphorylate glucose-6-phosphate with glucose-6-phosphatase.

Glycogenesis and glycogenolysis occur in both liver and skeletal muscle. Liver glycogen is broken down to maintain blood glucose during fasting, while skeletal muscle glycogen is broken down to supply glucose for muscle during exercise. Skeletal muscle lacks glucose-6-phosphatase, so its glucose remains phosphorylated and cannot leave the muscle cell.

Insulin is released when blood glucose is high and stimulates glycogenesis. Glucagon acts when blood glucose is low and stimulates liver glycogenolysis, releasing glucose into blood so other cells can take it up and use it in glycolysis.

## Pentose phosphate pathway

The pentose phosphate pathway, or hexose monophosphate shunt, diverts glucose-6-phosphate from glycolysis to make NADPH, ribose-5-phosphate, and glycolytic intermediates. It is a cytoplasmic pathway with an irreversible oxidative phase followed by a non-oxidative phase of reversible reactions.

The oxidative phase makes NADPH and ribulose-5-phosphate, from which ribose-5-phosphate is derived. The non-oxidative phase forms ribose-5-phosphate and glycolytic intermediates. Ribose-5-phosphate is used to synthesize nucleotides, while other carbohydrate intermediates can return to glycolysis.

NADPH serves as an important reducing agent in anabolic processes, especially fatty acid synthesis, and helps neutralize reactive oxygen species. Glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase is the first enzyme and primary regulatory point of the pentose phosphate pathway. NADPH negatively feeds back to inhibit glucose-6-phosphate dehydrogenase, and enzyme deficiency limits the ability of red blood cells to eliminate reactive oxygen species.
