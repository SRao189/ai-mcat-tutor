# Nucleic Acids

## Phosphate ATP and nucleotides

Phosphoric acid is an inorganic acid with the potential to donate three protons. Its three pKa values are 2.1, 7.2, and 12.4. At physiological pH, phosphoric acid is significantly dissociated and exists largely in anionic form. In extracellular fluid, the common species are hydrogen phosphate and dihydrogen phosphate.

Phosphate is also called orthophosphate. Two orthophosphates bound by an anhydride linkage form pyrophosphate, and the P-O-P bond in pyrophosphate is a high-energy phosphate bond because pyrophosphate hydrolysis is thermodynamically very favorable. The Delta G degrees for pyrophosphate hydrolysis is about -7 kcal/mol, and the actual cellular Delta G is about -12 kcal/mol.

Phosphate anhydride bonds store energy for three reasons. Linked phosphates repel each other strongly through their negative charges. Orthophosphate has more resonance forms and lower free energy than linked phosphates. Orthophosphate also interacts more favorably with water than linked phosphates.

Nucleotides are the building blocks of RNA and DNA. Each nucleotide contains a ribose or deoxyribose sugar, a purine or pyrimidine base joined to carbon 1 of the sugar ring, and one, two, or three phosphate units joined to carbon 5 of the sugar ring.

ATP is adenosine triphosphate, a ribonucleotide and the universal short-term energy storage molecule. Energy from oxidation of foodstuffs is immediately stored in ATP phosphoanhydride bonds, and this energy powers cellular processes or can be used to synthesize longer-term energy storage molecules such as glucose or fats. Other nucleotides can be used as energy less often; GTP is used in protein synthesis and UTP activates glucose-1-phosphate in glycogenesis.

## DNA nucleotide structure

DNA is deoxyribonucleic acid and RNA is ribonucleic acid. They are called nucleic acids because they are found in the nucleus and contain many acidic phosphate groups.

The building block of DNA is a deoxyribonucleoside 5-prime triphosphate, or dNTP. Deoxyribonucleotides have three components: deoxyribose, an aromatic nitrogenous base, and phosphate groups. Deoxyribose differs from ribose because the 2-prime hydroxyl is missing.

The DNA bases are adenine, guanine, cytosine, and thymine. Adenine and guanine are purines with a double-ring structure. Cytosine and thymine are pyrimidines with a single six-membered ring. Uracil is a pyrimidine found in RNA instead of thymine.

A nucleoside is ribose or deoxyribose linked to a purine or pyrimidine through a beta-N-glycosidic linkage at carbon 1. Nucleotides are phosphate esters of nucleosides, with one, two, or three phosphate groups joined to the sugar ring through the 5-prime hydroxyl group. Deoxynucleoside triphosphates are abbreviated dNTPs, where N is replaced by A, G, C, or T.

The sugar-phosphate portion of a nucleotide is the invariant backbone of nucleic acid, while the base is the variable portion. DNA has four different dNTPs that differ only in the aromatic base.

## Polynucleotides and base pairing

Nucleotides in nucleic acids are covalently linked by phosphodiester bonds between the 3-prime hydroxyl group of one sugar and the 5-prime phosphate group of the next sugar. A polymer of several nucleotides is an oligonucleotide, and a polymer of many nucleotides is a polynucleotide. Since the base is the unique part of a nucleotide, polynucleotide sequence is abbreviated by listing the bases in the chain.

The end of a polynucleotide with a free 5-prime phosphate group is written first, and the chain is written in the 5-prime to 3-prime direction.

In double-stranded DNA, two long polynucleotide chains hydrogen-bond in antiparallel orientation, meaning the 5-prime end of one chain is paired with the 3-prime end of the other. Hydrogen bonds form between bases on adjacent chains: adenine pairs with thymine, and guanine pairs with cytosine. Each base pair contains one purine and one pyrimidine, so AT and GC pairs occupy the same amount of space in the double helix.

The GC pair has three hydrogen bonds, while the AT pair has two. Two DNA strands are complementary if their bases can hydrogen bond when the strands are oriented antiparallel. Chargaff's rule states that A equals T, G equals C, and A plus G equals T plus C.

## DNA double helix stability and dimensions

The Watson-Crick model describes cellular DNA as a right-handed double helix held together by hydrogen bonds and hydrophobic forces between bases. DNA in the nucleus is double-stranded rather than a single long polynucleotide.

Annealing or hybridization is the binding of complementary DNA strands into a double-stranded structure. Melting or denaturation is strand separation. Tm is the temperature at which a DNA solution is 50 percent melted. AT-rich DNA has a lower Tm than GC-rich DNA because AT pairs have two hydrogen bonds while GC pairs have three.

The DNA double helix has bases on the interior and the ribose-phosphate backbone on the exterior. It is stabilized by van der Waals interactions between stacked bases and by hydrophobic interactions between bases. The bases lie perpendicular to the length of the DNA molecule and are stacked 3.4 angstroms apart. The helix completes a full turn every 34 angstroms, or every 10 base pairs, and the width of the double helix is always 20 angstroms.

## Chromosome packaging centromeres and telomeres

An organism's complete genetic information is its genome. Eukaryotic genomes are composed of several large linear pieces of double-stranded DNA, and each piece is a chromosome. Humans have 46 chromosomes, with 23 inherited from each parent. Prokaryotic genomes are composed of a single circular chromosome, while viral genomes may be linear or circular DNA or RNA.

DNA gyrase compacts prokaryotic circular chromosomes by using ATP to twist the circular molecule. The twists created by DNA gyrase are supercoils because they are coils of a structure that is already coiled.

Eukaryotic DNA is wrapped around globular histone proteins. DNA wrapped around histone octamers forms nucleosomes, giving a beads-on-a-string appearance before complete packing. A nucleosome is DNA wrapped around an octamer made of two units each of H2A, H2B, H3, and H4. Linker DNA is the double-helical DNA between nucleosomes and is bound by a linker histone. Fully packed DNA is chromatin.

Heterochromatin is darker, denser, rich in repeats, and more compact. Euchromatin is lighter, less dense, and has higher transcription rates because looser packing makes DNA more accessible to enzymes and proteins.

A centromere is the chromosome region where spindle fibers attach during cell division through kinetochores. Centromeres are made of heterochromatin and repetitive DNA sequences. Chromosomes have short p arms and long q arms, and centromere position defines the ratio between them.

The ends of linear chromosomes are telomeres. Telomeres contain distinct nucleotide sequences repeated 50 to several hundred times; the repeated unit is usually 6 to 8 base pairs long and guanine-rich. Many vertebrates, including humans and mice, have the repeat 5-prime-TTAGGG-3-prime. Telomeres prevent chromosome deterioration and prevent fusion with neighboring chromosomes, acting as disposable buffers at chromosome ends. Most prokaryotes have circular genomes and do not contain telomeres.

## RNA types and chemical differences

RNA differs chemically from DNA in three important ways: RNA is single-stranded except in some viruses, RNA contains uracil instead of thymine, and RNA contains ribose rather than 2-prime deoxyribose.

The 2-prime hydroxyl in RNA makes RNA less stable because it can nucleophilically attack the backbone phosphate group, causing hydrolysis when the remainder of the chain acts as a leaving group. DNA lacks a 2-prime hydroxyl, so this cannot occur in DNA.

Messenger RNA, or mRNA, is coding RNA that carries genetic information to the ribosome for translation into protein. Messenger RNA is continually produced and degraded according to the cell's need for the encoded protein, which is a major way cells regulate protein amounts. In eukaryotes, the first RNA transcribed from DNA is heterogeneous nuclear RNA, or hnRNA, and processing events such as capping, tail addition, and splicing are required to make mature mRNA. Prokaryotes do not process primary transcripts, so hnRNA is found only in eukaryotes.

Non-coding RNA is functional RNA that is not translated into protein. The two major MCAT types are transfer RNA and ribosomal RNA. Transfer RNA carries amino acids from the cytoplasm to the ribosome and helps translate the genetic code. Ribosomal RNA is the major component of the ribosome; humans have 18S, 5.8S, 28S, and 5S rRNAs, and one rRNA provides the catalytic function of the ribosome.

Catalytic RNAs are called ribozymes because they can perform specific biochemical reactions like protein enzymes. Other non-coding RNAs include small nuclear RNAs in spliceosome snRNP complexes, microRNAs and small interfering RNAs in RNA interference, and long non-coding RNAs that regulate transcription, splicing, translation, imprinting, and X-chromosome inactivation.
