# PCSK9 RNAi Journal Article

## Background and Citation

This Journal Article Exercise analyzes Kevin Fitzgerald, Suellen White, Anna Borodovsky, Brian R. Bettencourt, Andrew Strahs, Valerie Clausen, Peter Wijngaard, Jay D. Horton, Jorg Taubel, Ashley Brooks, Chamikara Fernando, Robert S. Kauffman, David Kallend, Akshay Vaishnaw, and Amy Simon, "A Highly Durable RNAi Therapeutic Inhibitor of PCSK9," published in the New England Journal of Medicine, volume 376, pages 41-51, in 2017.

The article begins from the clinical problem that elevated LDL cholesterol is a major cardiovascular risk factor and that some at-risk patients continue to have elevated LDL despite statins or other lipid-lowering medications. PCSK9 is presented as a therapeutic target because it promotes lysosomal degradation of LDL receptors in hepatocytes, thereby increasing circulating LDL cholesterol.

Inclisiran, also called ALN-PCSsc, is described as a long-acting, subcutaneously delivered synthetic siRNA directed against PCSK9. The siRNA engages RNA interference by guiding RISC to cleave PCSK9 mRNA, decreasing availability of the transcript for translation and reducing PCSK9 protein levels. The trial assessed safety, side-effect profile, and pharmacodynamic effects of single or multiple subcutaneous doses.

## Study Methods

The study was a randomized, single-blind, placebo-controlled phase 1 study performed in two stages: a single-dose phase with ascending doses and a multiple-dose phase. It was conducted at two contract research sites in the United Kingdom. Data were collected by investigators and analyzed by Covance and Alnylam Pharmaceuticals.

Eligible participants had LDL cholesterol of at least 100 mg per deciliter and fasting triglycerides below 400 mg per deciliter. Participants taking statins had to be on a stable statin dose and regimen for at least 30 days before screening, with no planned changes during the study. Participants were monitored for safety and pharmacodynamic endpoints, including plasma PCSK9 and serum LDL cholesterol.

In the single-dose phase, participants received placebo or inclisiran at 25, 100, 300, 500, or 800 mg. In the multiple-dose phase, participants received placebo or inclisiran regimens that included 125 mg weekly, 250 mg every two weeks, 300 mg monthly, or 500 mg monthly, with some 300 mg and 500 mg cohorts also taking statins. Statistical comparisons used repeated-measures ANCOVA, with p < 0.05 considered significant.

## Results and Discussion

The article reports that all adverse events were mild or moderate in severity, with no treatment discontinuations due to adverse events and no serious adverse events. In the single-dose phase, inclisiran doses of 300 mg or more significantly reduced PCSK9 at day 84 compared with placebo. In the multiple-dose phase, all inclisiran regimens significantly reduced PCSK9 at day 84 compared with placebo.

For LDL cholesterol, single doses of 100 mg or more significantly reduced LDL at day 84 compared with placebo. In the multiple-dose phase, LDL reductions were significant at day 84 for all inclisiran regimens except the 125 mg weekly for 4 weeks cohort. The article also reports decreases in total cholesterol, non-HDL cholesterol, and apolipoprotein B in inclisiran-treated participants.

The discussion concludes that inclisiran reduced circulating PCSK9 and LDL cholesterol with effects that persisted for at least 180 days after initiation of treatment. It contrasts inclisiran with anti-PCSK9 antibodies: antibodies bind extracellular PCSK9, while inclisiran inhibits PCSK9 protein synthesis specifically in the liver. The authors state that the phase 1 results are preliminary and should be confirmed in larger and longer clinical trials.

## ORCWS: Organism and Organization

The Princeton Review solution identifies the organisms as humans: 21 women and 48 men. The participants were treated with inclisiran or placebo to test whether inclisiran lowers LDL levels.

The solution highlights the entry criterion that participants had LDL levels of 100 mg per deciliter or higher. It identifies multiple groups: a single-dose inclisiran group, a multidose inclisiran group, placebo groups, and multidose subgroups split by whether participants also received a statin. It describes the study as "long-ish," with the single-dose group followed for approximately six months and the multidose group for approximately seven months. It also notes that the sample size was relatively small for a human study.

## ORCWS: Results

The Princeton Review solution says the results are presented in tables and graphs. It identifies time, inclisiran versus placebo, and statin versus no statin as independent variables.

The dependent variables are LDL levels and PCSK9 levels. The solution describes PCSK9 as a serine protease that can raise LDL levels by destroying LDL receptors. It summarizes the results as statistically significant reductions in LDL and PCSK9 for single-dose inclisiran at doses of 300 mg or higher, and for the multidose protocol as presented in the exercise solution.

The solution treats the results as causative within the experimental design because single-dose and multidose protocols used placebo groups, and the effect seen in inclisiran-treated participants was not seen in placebo participants.

## ORCWS: Control and Comparison

The control group was placebo. Both the single-dose and multidose portions of the study included participants who received placebo instead of inclisiran.

The experimental groups differed by dose schedule and dose amount. The single-dose phase used one dose ranging from 25 mg to 800 mg. The multidose phase used fewer dose sizes but added the comparison of statin cotherapy versus no statin cotherapy. The study was single-blinded.

## ORCWS: Weirdness

The Princeton Review solution flags an apparent dose-schedule wrinkle. In the single-dose protocol, effectiveness was demonstrated starting at 300 mg. In the multidose protocol, the lowest total dose discussed in the solution was lower than that single-dose threshold, yet multidose treatment was described as effective in the exercise solution.

For MCAT critical analysis, the lesson is to separate single-dose and multidose protocols instead of comparing dose numbers as if schedule, timing, and repeated exposure did not matter.

## ORCWS: Statistics

The study used statistical analysis. The article reports repeated-measures ANCOVA and a p < 0.05 significance threshold, while the Princeton Review solution points students to p-values, standard errors on means, and graph error bars.

The exercise solution identifies placebo branches as not producing statistically significant effects. The article-level results also make clear that not every inclisiran condition was significant for every endpoint: for example, LDL cholesterol reduction in the 125 mg weekly multiple-dose cohort was not significant at day 84 compared with placebo in the article text.

## Interpreting the Data

The Princeton Review interpretation is that inclisiran could reduce LDL levels in people with LDL levels above 100 mg per deciliter, with or without statin use. The solution says this interpretation is supported by statistically significant drops in LDL and PCSK9 in treated groups.

The source also asks students to recognize limitations. The Princeton Review solution notes that the population was mostly male and mostly white, and that a larger sample with broader demographic representation would strengthen the study. The article discussion similarly warns that the trial was single-blind, had a limited number of participants, and included mainly healthy participants with relatively normal lipid profiles apart from LDL of at least 100 mg per deciliter.

## Final Summary

Fitzgerald and colleagues tested inclisiran, an siRNA therapeutic targeting PCSK9, in a randomized, single-blind, placebo-controlled phase 1 trial. Participants with LDL cholesterol of at least 100 mg per deciliter received placebo or inclisiran in single-dose or multidose regimens, with some multidose participants also taking statins.

The article reports no serious adverse events, significant PCSK9 reductions for single doses of at least 300 mg and for all multidose regimens, and significant LDL reductions for single doses of at least 100 mg and for most multidose regimens except the 125 mg weekly cohort. The ORCWS analysis emphasizes the control comparisons, the dose and statin subgroup structure, the small and demographically limited sample, and the need for larger follow-up trials.
